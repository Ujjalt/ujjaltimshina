# Returning to the Flow

<div class="post-meta">
3 June 2026 · Mathematics Laboratory · IIIT Delhi
</div>

For the past several months, mathematics had become more than a subject of study for me. It had become a rhythm. I was writing every day, proving lemmas, exploring ideas, and spending long hours immersed in a state of concentration where time itself seemed irrelevant. The work felt natural. The mathematics flowed.

Then, on 15 May 2026, I left the campus for a thirteen-day vacation and returned on 28 May. The break was not unproductive. I spent time with my family, met old friends, attended a convocation ceremony, and shared moments with people who matter deeply to me. Mathematics never completely left my mind. I kept thinking about a research problem and occasionally noted down ideas, but there was a difference: I was not writing. Whenever a thought appeared, I opened Overleaf and typed it. For nearly two weeks, the pen remained untouched.

Today, sitting in the laboratory, I picked up a pen and a sheet of paper and tried to write mathematics again. The experience was unexpectedly uncomfortable. My handwriting looked unfamiliar, the symbols felt awkward, and even the way I structured arguments on paper seemed clumsy. For a brief moment, I felt as though I had forgotten how to communicate mathematically.

Of course, I have not forgotten mathematics itself. The definitions remain where they always were, and the ideas have not disappeared. Yet mathematical practice is a craft as much as it is knowledge. Like playing an instrument, it requires continuous engagement. A few weeks away from the routine were enough to make me feel disconnected from habits that once felt automatic.

As I write this, I am sitting alone in the laboratory. It is summer in Delhi. The institute has already switched off the air conditioning for the evening, and the room is warm. The silence is almost complete. On my desk lies a lemma that I proposed some time ago. I am trying to prove it, although I do not yet know whether the statement is actually true. Perhaps tomorrow I will discover a flaw. Perhaps a proof will eventually emerge. Time will decide.

What I do know is that I am no longer working in the same flow that I experienced before leaving campus. The pace feels slower. The focus comes and goes. Every argument demands more effort than it once did.

## Do I Regret Going Home?

Naturally, this raises a question: do I regret going home?

No.

I do not regret the conversations with my family. I do not regret meeting friends. I do not regret attending the convocation ceremony. I certainly do not regret spending time with one of the best friends of my life.

Mathematics is a central purpose of my life, but it is not the entirety of life. The people we love, the relationships we nurture, and the memories we create are not interruptions to our work. They are among the reasons the work matters at all.

## The Cost of Transition

What I am experiencing now is not failure. It is simply the cost of transition. The mind that left one rhythm is learning another again. The flow has not vanished; it has merely become distant. Like a river after a dry season, it waits to be rediscovered.

Perhaps every serious mathematician encounters such moments. We step away from our desks and return fearing that we have lost something essential. Yet what feels lost is often only dormant. The habits return. The concentration returns. The confidence returns. One page becomes two. One calculation becomes a proof. One proof becomes a programme of research.

Tonight, while searching for a path through a lemma whose truth remains uncertain, I remind myself that mathematics is not measured by a single day of productivity. It is measured over years of persistence. Flow comes and goes. Inspiration comes and goes. Confidence comes and goes. Purpose remains.

## Flow and Purpose

Perhaps that is the important distinction.

> **Flow is a state; purpose is a direction.**

A state may disappear for days or weeks, but a direction survives every interruption. As long as the direction remains clear, one can always return.

---

So I return—to the page, to the proof, and to the work.
