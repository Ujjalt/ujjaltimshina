<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">
  This is not about mathematics, research, or success. It is about the quiet moments that arrive when none of those things seem to make sense. It is about the small thoughts that keep me company while sitting in the lab, waiting for difficult days to pass.
</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  There are days when research refuses to move.

  On such days, I often stop looking at the mathematics for a few minutes.

  Instead, I look outside the window.

  Although I now spend my days in Delhi, my mind quietly walks back to the mountains where I grew up.

  The mountains have always been my greatest teachers.

  If you have never lived among them, you might imagine that the weather follows a schedule.

  It doesn't.

  A bright morning can suddenly disappear behind dark clouds.

  By noon, rain may fall so heavily that the roads vanish beneath sheets of water.

  You begin to think the entire day has been lost.

  Then, without warning, the clouds drift away.

  The sun returns as though nothing had happened.

  The valleys glow again.

  Children come outside.

  Birds begin singing.

  You smile for a while.

  And then, perhaps an hour later, another storm arrives.

  That is simply how mountains live.

  No one argues with the weather.

  Not because they have discovered some great philosophy,

  but because they have no choice.

  The rain does not ask for permission.

  The sun does not arrive because we wish for it.

  The clouds never consult our plans.

  They simply come and go.

  Perhaps life asks the same of us.

</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:22px; font-weight:bold; margin-bottom:20px; text-align:center;">
  Life Has Always Been Mountain Weather
</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  Somewhere along the way, I realized that life behaves in exactly the same manner.

  We often expect happiness to arrive and stay forever.

  We imagine that once we reach a certain destination—a degree, a job, a publication, a dream—the difficult weather will finally disappear.

  But that has never been the nature of life.

  Joy comes.

  Then uncertainty.

  Confidence visits for a while.

  Self-doubt follows quietly behind.

  Some mornings begin with hope and end with disappointment.

  Other days begin with disappointment and unexpectedly become beautiful before sunset.

  The weather keeps changing.

  So do we.

  Perhaps peace does not come from controlling the weather.

  Perhaps it comes from understanding that weather was never meant to stay still.

</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  During difficult times, people often search for the meaning of life.

  I have done the same.

  But every time I try to answer that question, another one appears.

  Why was I born?

  Why now?

  Why here?

  What happens after death?

  The truth is,

  I do not know.

  None of us remembers the moment we entered this world.

  And no one has returned after death to tell us what lies beyond it.

  We spend our entire lives standing quietly between two mysteries.

  One before our first breath.

  The other after our last.

  Since neither can be experienced while we are alive, perhaps it is alright to leave them where they belong.

  Not every mystery exists to be solved.

  Some exist simply to remind us how little we know.

  Instead of trying to understand everything beyond life, perhaps it is enough to understand life itself.

</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  When I sit at my desk in the laboratory—Seat D—I sometimes forget about deadlines for a few minutes.

  The papers remain open.

  Chalk dust rests quietly on the table.

  A notebook waits for another proof.

  Outside, the world continues without caring whether today's calculations succeed or fail.

  My thoughts travel elsewhere.

  They travel to rain touching pine trees.

  To mist covering distant hills.

  To the smell of wet earth after an afternoon storm.

  Those memories remind me that the world is much larger than one unsuccessful calculation.

  Mathematics will wait.

  The theorem is patient.

  Tomorrow's proof will not disappear simply because today was difficult.

</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  Over time I have discovered that the things I love during difficult days are surprisingly ordinary.

  <ul style="color:#e8f5e8;">
    <li>Watching rain without wishing it would stop.</li>
    <li>The brief appearance of sunlight after hours of dark clouds.</li>
    <li>A quiet cup of tea when nobody is speaking.</li>
    <li>The smell of old books resting on a wooden shelf.</li>
    <li>The silence of the laboratory after everyone has gone home.</li>
    <li>The sound of pages turning instead of endless notifications.</li>
    <li>A tiny mathematical idea that finally begins to make sense after refusing to do so for days.</li>
    <li>Remembering the mountains that taught me patience long before research ever did.</li>
  </ul>

  None of these things solve life's problems.

  They are too small for that.

  Yet somehow they remind me that difficult moments are only moments.

  Like mountain rain,

  they never stay forever.

</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  If the mountains have taught me one lesson, it is this:

  Never make permanent decisions because of temporary weather.

  Storms are convincing.

  They make us believe the sky has forgotten how to be blue.

  Yet every mountain knows something the storm does not.

  The clouds are only visitors.

  The sky was there before they arrived,

  and it will remain long after they leave.

  Life, I think, is much the same.

</div>

<div style="background:#0e2e0e; padding:18px 22px; border-radius:8px; color:#e8f5e8; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-size:16px; margin-bottom:20px;">

  As I finish writing these thoughts from my little corner of the lab—Seat D—the evening outside has become quieter.

  Someone is still writing on a whiteboard.

  Another student walks past carrying a stack of papers.

  The ceiling fan continues its slow rhythm.

  Life goes on in its ordinary way.

  Tomorrow may bring another difficult theorem.

  Or perhaps an unexpected breakthrough.

  I cannot predict either one.

  But then again,

  neither can the mountains predict their own weather.

  They simply stand.

  They welcome the rain.

  They welcome the sunshine.

  They endure every season without asking why one arrived before the other.

  Perhaps that is enough.

  Perhaps living is not about predicting the weather,

  but about learning to remain standing while it changes.

</div>
